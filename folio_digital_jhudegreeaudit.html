JHU Degree Audit

RE-DESIGNING THE JOHNS HOPKINS DEGREE-AUDITING SYSTEM

April 2015

Background

Purpose & Goal

To create a design for the degree-auditing system that is user-friendly, intuitive, and serves the purpose specifically desired from advisors, students, and professors.

Problems

The current degree-auditing system has never been a favored companion of professors, students, and academic/faculty advisors. The system is powerful and incredibly comprehensive, making it victim to aimless clicks, navigational confusion, and cluttered interfaces. The organization is standard - subcategories nested within countless more. Important information is often hidden in the cluttered branches, boxes, and distracting colors, and the comprehensive data is not presented clearly. In observing the navigation of the degree-auditing system by advisors, primarily, I had noted inexcusable inefficiency and apprehension in operating new tasks within the system, such as: creating exceptions for courses, creating new "goals," simply understanding a particular student's academic situation. Tedious navigation aside, the amount of time it takes to create an audit is excessive, and can be dramatically reduced with thoughtful analysis, organization, and a re-design of the system.

02 Brainstorming Process: Wireframing

I first started with a couple of preliminary sketches on paper, listing out what worked and didn't work with the current system. Some of my notes are included below:

    Pros:

  • Subcategories are nested, which is a system generally
    understood and people are familiar with
  • The X's and checkmarks clearly indicate which
    requirements are completed and which are not
  • The different categories are separated according to color

    Cons:

  • An excess of subcategories makes it inefficient for users to
    identify and seek out targeted information
  • Many listed requirements are redundant, and need to be
    read carefully in order to realize overlaps in
    requirements (again, an organizational shortcoming)

I created a design focused on simplicity. I wanted to make sure the most important information was presented. In other words, at degree-auditing meetings, what information is generally targeted, and what do professors, students, and advisors look for in a degree audit? Understanding the purpose of the degree-audit in a practical college setting is important for identifying an effective redesign.

03 Creating a Mockup

Here I took to the web and created a simplified, more user-friendly interface for the degree-auditing system. I decided to include only information that was commonly used, in order to preserve a clean and unintimidating presence. Although I did not include the details of each course (such as semester, year, etc.), there is potential for the integration of such information, if desired. This information could be unveiled upon the hovering of the cursor over each specific course. Generally, students create audits to see progress in their degree requirements, and to see what requirements are left in order to graduate. Excess information only gets in the way. Professors and advisors use audits for similar purposes--to help students understand their academic goals and progress--but with the additional power to create course exceptions, manage transferred credits, and ensure completion of the requirements. This design is catered for these needs--the overview is intended to direct the user to a quick glance of the major/minor requirements, while what is nested underneath each goal (major/minor degree), is a record of all the courses that have been completed (in blue) and still need to be completed (in red). The two columns beside the course listings display the corresponding credits and grade for the courses. As for the aesthetic design element, I chose to create a design that remains cohesive with the Johns Hopkins University theme, which is congealed from a number of JHU platforms:Main Page,Krieger School of Arts & Sciences,The HUB, andThe Library. Of course, there is still much to be smoothed over in the process of this design. Many minor organizational questions remain to be probed, but I believe this redesign is an important preliminary step in dissolving the complications of degree-auditing. I believe this redesign will greatly improve the user-experience for all, but most importantly, for those who use it most.
